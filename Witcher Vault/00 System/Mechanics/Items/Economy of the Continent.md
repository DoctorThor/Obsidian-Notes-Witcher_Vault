### A 5e Economic Framework for the Witcher Campaign

---

## Design Anchor

All prices in this document are calibrated to a single fixed point: **an 800 crown contract is moderate pay for a level 3–4 party**. This is not exceptional money — it is a well-paying job from a serious employer. Everything else in the economy is built outward from that anchor.

A moderate contract should cover roughly 2–4 weeks of full party expenses. That puts party weekly running costs at **200–400 crowns**, or **50–100 crowns per character per week** at baseline. All goods, services, lifestyle costs, and reward tables follow from this.

---

## Currency

All prices use **Novigrad Crowns (NC)** as the base unit. The Novigrad crown is the most widely accepted currency on the Continent — merchants, innkeepers, and mercenaries across the Northern Kingdoms take it without question.

### Currency Conversion

| Currency | Region | Value in NC |
|---|---|---|
| Novigrad Crown (NC) | Northern Kingdoms standard | 1 NC |
| Cintran Silver | Cintra & Verden | 1 NC |
| Temerian Oren | Temeria | 0.9 NC |
| Redanian Ducat | Redania | 1.1 NC |
| Nilfgaardian Florin | Nilfgaard & occupied territories | 1.3 NC |
| Skellige Mark | Skellige Isles | 0.8 NC (often traded in kind) |
| Mahakam Token | Dwarven communities | 1 NC (dwarves only accept at face value from trusted parties) |

Regional pricing layers on top of this baseline in separate documents per region. These base prices assume a neutral Northern Kingdoms town in peacetime.

## The Economic Ladder

| Monthly Income | Who Earns It |
|---|---|
| 20–50 NC | Destitute — beggars, displaced peasants, refugees |
| 60–120 NC | Poor — farm laborers, unskilled workers, servants |
| 130–250 NC | Working — skilled tradespeople, soldiers, craftsmen |
| 260–500 NC | Comfortable — merchants, minor officials, inn owners |
| 500–1,200 NC | Prosperous — successful merchants, hedge knights, witchers (variable) |
| 1,200–3,000 NC | Wealthy — minor nobility, guild masters, established mages |
| 3,000+ NC | Powerful — major nobility, court sorcerers, crime lords |

A witcher pulling regular contracts sits in the Prosperous bracket — but only if the work is consistent. A dry month drops them fast.

**The non-human penalty:** The elf and dwarves in this party will routinely be offered 10–20% less for their labor and charged 10–20% more for goods in hostile human settlements. This is a narrative rule enforced situationally, not a flat mechanical tax every session.

## Lifestyle & Weekly Drain

Lifestyle costs are paid **weekly**. At the end of each in-world week, each character pays their tier cost. If they cannot pay, consequences follow.

### Lifestyle Tiers

#### Wretched (0 NC/week)
Sleeping rough — barns, abandoned buildings, haystacks, ditches.
- **Mechanical effect:** DC 10 Constitution saving throw each morning. On a failure, gain one level of exhaustion. Resets after one night at a better lifestyle.
- **Social effect:** Disadvantage on Charisma checks where appearance matters. NPCs treat the character as a vagrant or criminal.
- **Non-human note:** Elves and dwarves living rough are assumed to be fugitives by most settlement gates. Expect stops, searches, and demands for papers.

#### Squalid (10 NC/week)
A shared flophouse room, one meal a day, no luxuries.
- **Mechanical effect:** None, but equipment degrades faster — see Equipment Maintenance.
- **Social effect:** Disadvantage on Charisma checks in formal or wealthy contexts.

#### Poor (25 NC/week)
A private room in a cheap inn or boarding house. Two meals. Enough to stay functional.
- **Mechanical effect:** None.
- **Social effect:** No modifier. The invisible baseline.

#### Modest (50 NC/week)
A decent room, three meals, a bath twice a week, stabling if needed. You look like someone with a trade.
- **Mechanical effect:** Advantage on Charisma (Persuasion) checks with working-class NPCs.
- **Social effect:** You move through most social environments without standing out.

#### Comfortable (100 NC/week)
A good inn room, regular meals, pressed clothes, horse stabled. You look like someone who matters.
- **Mechanical effect:** Advantage on Charisma checks with merchants, minor officials, and prosperous NPCs.
- **Social effect:** Guards are less likely to stop you. Innkeepers talk more freely.

#### Wealthy (250 NC/week)
Private lodgings, servants, tailored clothing, the best table. You look like minor nobility.
- **Mechanical effect:** Advantage on Charisma checks with nobility and court officials. Disadvantage on Charisma checks with working-class NPCs who distrust wealth.
- **Social effect:** Doors open. Other doors close. People remember you — not always useful.

#### Aristocratic (600+ NC/week)
Included for completeness and aspiration. Not currently relevant for this party.

## Equipment Maintenance & Degradation

### Weekly Maintenance Cost

| Equipment Type | Weekly Maintenance |
|---|---|
| No armor, simple weapons | 6 NC |
| Light armor, martial weapons | 15 NC |
| Medium armor, martial weapons | 25 NC |
| Heavy armor, martial weapons | 40 NC |
| Witcher equipment (silver + steel + armor) | 50 NC |
| Shield (add to above) | 6 NC |

**Consequence of non-payment:** Equipment unmaintained for two consecutive weeks becomes **worn** — a −1 penalty to attack rolls or AC until repaired. Repair costs double the missed maintenance.

**Silver weapons** require a skilled blacksmith. In a settlement without one, silver maintenance costs 3× normal rate and finding the right smith may itself be a side quest.

### Repair Costs

| Damage Level | Cost |
|---|---|
| Minor (chips, dulling) | 15 NC |
| Moderate (bent, cracked) | 45–90 NC |
| Severe (broken, destroyed) | 50–80% of replacement cost |
| Silver sword repair | Minimum 120 NC, requires specialist |

## Reward Scaling

### The Core Principle

A completed contract should cover **2–4 weeks of full party expenses** — enough to matter without making the next job optional. At roughly 300 NC/week for a party of four, that puts the sweet spot at **600–1,200 NC per moderate contract**, with 800 NC sitting comfortably in the middle.

### Contract Rewards by Threat Level

Base rates reflect what a **serious, solvent employer** pays. Village headmen and frightened farmers pay 40–60% of these rates — they offer what they can scrape together, not what the job is worth. Skilled negotiation (DC varies, 12–16 depending on employer) can push rates up 15–25%.

| Threat Level | Creature Examples | Base Reward |
|---|---|---|
| Minor (CR 1/4–1/2) | Drowner, nekker, ghoul | 100–200 NC |
| Low (CR 1–2) | Specter, leshen imp, young wyvern | 200–400 NC |
| Moderate (CR 3–5) | Noon wraith, werewolf, foglet, Aldric | 600–1,000 NC |
| Serious (CR 6–8) | Water hag, succubus, higher vampire fledgling | 1,000–1,800 NC |
| Dangerous (CR 9–12) | Fiend, powerful wraith, bruxa | 1,800–3,200 NC |
| Legendary (CR 13+) | Archgriffin, ancient vampire, powerful fiend | 3,200–8,000 NC |

**Institutional premium:** Military commanders, court officials, and wealthy merchants pay 1.5–2× the base rate. They are buying discretion and reliability, not just muscle. Varro's 800 NC commission is mid-range for an institutional employer at this threat level — generous but not extravagant by his standards.

**Village rate:** A frightened village headman scraping together payment for a monster that's been killing livestock offers 40–60% of base. The party may take it out of necessity or negotiate up with a successful Charisma check.

**Non-human penalty:** Hostile employers offer 15–20% less to non-human party members as baseline. DC 13 Charisma (Intimidation or Persuasion) negates this for that transaction.

## Magic Item Pricing

Magic items are not sold in shops. They are found, inherited, won, or traded in very specific circumstances. When they do appear for sale, the seller is almost always a fence, a private collector, or someone desperate.

### Availability by Settlement Size

| Settlement | What's Available |
|---|---|
| Village / hamlet | Nothing. Maybe a lucky charm that doesn't work. |
| Small town | Occasionally: minor consumables from a hedgewitch or herbalist |
| Large town | Potions and oils from a known dealer — rare, not stocked regularly |
| City (Cintra, Oxenfurt, Vizima) | Consumables reliably; permanent items by appointment |
| Novigrad | Most things, if you know who to ask and can afford it |

### Pricing Framework

| Category | Price Range |
|---|---|
| Consumables — Minor | Basic healing potion, swallow, single-use scroll (1st level) | 120–250 NC |
| Consumables — Standard | Cat, tawny owl, thunderbolt, 2nd level scroll | 250–550 NC |
| Consumables — Rare | White honey, superior healing, 3rd level scroll | 550–1,300 NC |
| Permanent — Common | +1 weapon or armor, minor trinket | 960–1,900 NC |
| Permanent — Uncommon | Named weapon, armor with specific ability, useful ring | 1,900–4,800 NC |
| Permanent — Rare | Significant items, powerful enchantments, Elder Race artifacts | 4,800–12,800 NC |
| Permanent — Very Rare | Campaign-defining items, legendary weapons | 12,800–38,000 NC |
| Permanent — Legendary | Not for sale. Full stop. | — |

**The fence discount:** Stolen or unexplainable items sell for 25–40% of the above values.

**The Elder Race premium:** Elven or dwarven-made items command a 20–40% premium from human collectors. The PC elf or dwarves are offered them at baseline or below — cultural items returning to their people.

## The Non-Human Economic Reality

### Structural Disadvantages

**Employment:** Non-humans are legally barred from certain professions in some Northern Kingdom cities. Informal exclusion is widespread everywhere else.

**Property:** In several jurisdictions, non-humans cannot own property outright. They rent, always, at rates humans set.

**Banking:** Cintran banking houses extend credit to dwarves at standard rates (reliable reputation) but not to elves. Non-human borrowers face 5–10% higher interest regardless.

**Contracts:** A non-human's verbal agreement has less legal standing in human courts. Written contracts are essential — and insisting on them is viewed with suspicion.

### The Underground Economy

The non-human community operates a parallel economy accessible to the party's elf and dwarves — and to the witcher only with a proper introduction.

**What it offers:**
- Fair prices from non-human vendors
- Black market access to restricted items
- Information networks humans don't have access to
- Safe houses and discreet lodging
- Fencing without the human criminal markup

**What it costs:**
- Community obligation — help is expected in return
- Political entanglement — the underground has its own factions and debts
- Risk — discovery by human authorities carries serious consequences

## Quick Reference — Weekly Party Budget

Worked example for a party of four at Level 3–4 operating from a moderate town base:

| Expense | Weekly Cost (per character) |
|---|---|
| Modest lifestyle | 50 NC |
| Equipment maintenance (medium armor + weapons) | 25 NC |
| Horse upkeep (if applicable) | 35 NC |
| Incidentals (tolls, small bribes, sundries) | ~15 NC |
| **Weekly baseline per character** | **~90–125 NC** |
| **Party of four, weekly** | **~360–500 NC** |

An 800 NC contract split four ways is 200 NC each — roughly 1.5–2 weeks of personal expenses. The party keeps working. They are never quite comfortable enough to stop.

## DM Guidance

**Don't nickle-and-dime.** Track weekly lifestyle and maintenance. Let incidentals flow narratively. Pull out specific prices only when a purchase or negotiation matters to the story.

**Let scarcity create decisions.** The best economic moments are not "can we afford this" but "we can afford one of these two things — which do we choose."

**The non-human tax is narrative, not mechanical.** Apply it when it adds texture — when it makes the elf's jaw tighten, when the dwarf decides whether to push back or swallow it, when the witcher decides whether to intervene.

**Money should matter at every level.** If the party accumulates more than two months of expenses without spending it, introduce a meaningful sink — an equipment upgrade, a contact who needs a loan, a political situation coin can resolve, property in a place they'll return to.

**The village rate versus the institutional rate is a story tool.** A job that pays 200 NC instead of 800 NC isn't just less money — it tells the party something about who hired them and what they're worth to that person. Use the disparity deliberately.
